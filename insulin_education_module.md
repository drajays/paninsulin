# INSULIN AND DIABETES SELF-MANAGEMENT EDUCATION MODULE

**For adults, adolescents, families and caregivers**  
**Evidence review date: July 2026**

## Important safety note
This is a structured education resource, not a substitute for an individualized prescription or emergency assessment. It covers type 1, type 2, gestational and other diabetes, but recommendations differ by age, pregnancy, kidney/liver function, medicines, resources and comorbidities. Do not start, stop or change insulin, glucose-lowering medicines, pump settings or pregnancy treatment without the treating diabetes team. In an emergency—severe breathing difficulty, confusion, seizure, unconsciousness, persistent vomiting, suspected diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), severe dehydration, chest pain or stroke symptoms—seek emergency care.

Units are shown in **mg/dL** with **mmol/L** in parentheses where useful. 1 mmol/L glucose is approximately 18 mg/dL. Individual targets must be agreed with the healthcare team.

## Evidence basis
The module is aligned primarily with the **American Diabetes Association (ADA) Standards of Care in Diabetes—2026**, the **International Diabetes Federation (IDF) Global Clinical Practice Recommendations for Type 2 Diabetes Management—2025**, and relevant guidance/consensus from the ADA/EASD/JBDS/AACE/Diabetes Technology Society, Endocrine Society/European Society of Endocrinology, ACOG, KDIGO, IWGDF, ISPAD, CDC and WHO. It is written in patient-facing language; it intentionally avoids giving individualized insulin doses.

# 1. Diabetes—an introduction

**Learning focus:** Understand what diabetes is, why glucose rises, what treatment aims to achieve and how to take an active role in care.

### 1. What is diabetes?
**Answer:** Diabetes is a group of conditions in which blood glucose remains too high because the body makes too little insulin, does not use insulin effectively, or both. Chronic high glucose can injure blood vessels and nerves.

### 2. What is glucose?
**Answer:** Glucose is a major fuel for the body. It comes mainly from carbohydrate-containing foods and is carried in the blood to cells. Insulin helps glucose move from blood into many cells for energy or storage.

### 3. What is insulin?
**Answer:** Insulin is a hormone made by pancreatic beta cells. It lowers blood glucose by helping cells use glucose and by limiting glucose release from the liver. People with type 1 diabetes need replacement insulin to live.

### 4. What are the main types of diabetes?
**Answer:** Type 1 involves autoimmune or other severe loss of insulin production; type 2 involves insulin resistance with progressive beta-cell dysfunction; gestational diabetes is hyperglycemia first recognized during pregnancy. Other specific forms also occur.

### 5. Is diabetes contagious or caused simply by eating sugar?
**Answer:** No. Diabetes is not contagious. Type 2 risk is influenced by genetics, body composition, age, activity, sleep, medicines and social conditions. Sugary foods can contribute excess carbohydrate or calories, but they are not the sole cause.

### 6. What symptoms can high glucose cause?
**Answer:** Frequent urination, excessive thirst, blurred vision, fatigue, recurrent infections and unintentional weight loss may occur. Type 2 diabetes may have few symptoms, so screening is important. DKA symptoms require urgent assessment.

### 7. How is diabetes diagnosed?
**Answer:** Common diagnostic thresholds are A1C ≥6.5%, fasting plasma glucose ≥126 mg/dL (7.0 mmol/L), 2-hour glucose ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) during a 75-g oral glucose tolerance test, or random glucose ≥200 mg/dL with classic symptoms. Testing is usually confirmed unless hyperglycemia is unequivocal.

### 8. What is prediabetes?
**Answer:** Prediabetes means glucose is above normal but below the diabetes diagnostic range. It signals increased risk of type 2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease. Weight, food pattern, activity, sleep and—when appropriate—medication can reduce progression risk.

### 9. What is the usual glucose target for a nonpregnant adult?
**Answer:** For many nonpregnant adults, ADA commonly uses A1C <7%, premeal glucose 80–130 mg/dL (4.4–7.2 mmol/L) and peak after-meal glucose <180 mg/dL (10.0 mmol/L). Targets must be individualized, especially in children, older adults, pregnancy or high hypoglycemia risk.

### 10. What does A1C mean?
**Answer:** A1C estimates average glucose exposure over roughly the previous 2–3 months, with greater weighting to recent weeks. It does not show daily highs and lows and may be unreliable with anemia, hemoglobin variants, altered red-cell survival, pregnancy or some kidney conditions.

### 11. Why can glucose vary even when I follow my plan?
**Answer:** Food composition, insulin timing, activity, stress hormones, sleep, infection, menstrual or pregnancy changes, medicines, injection-site problems and device error can alter glucose. Look for patterns with your team rather than blaming yourself for single readings.

### 12. Does everyone with diabetes need insulin?
**Answer:** Everyone with type 1 diabetes requires insulin replacement. Some people with type 2 or gestational diabetes need insulin temporarily or long term; others use non-insulin medicines and lifestyle measures. Severe symptomatic hyperglycemia may require insulin promptly.

### 13. What is diabetes self-management education and support (DSMES)?
**Answer:** DSMES is structured, ongoing teaching and problem-solving support from trained professionals. It covers food, medicines, insulin, monitoring, activity, sick days, risk reduction and emotional health. It should be offered at diagnosis, during life changes and when goals or treatment change.

### 14. Who should be on the diabetes care team?
**Answer:** Depending on need, the team may include a primary clinician, endocrinologist, diabetes care and education specialist, dietitian, pharmacist, eye professional, foot specialist, kidney specialist, obstetric clinician and mental-health professional. The person with diabetes remains the central member.

### 15. What does “person-centred care” mean?
**Answer:** It means decisions combine evidence with your goals, culture, work, food access, finances, abilities, preferences, safety and support. Shared decisions are reviewed over time; there is no single “perfect” diabetes plan for everyone.

### 16. What is the difference between hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia?
**Answer:** Hyperglycemia is glucose above the agreed target; persistent or severe elevation can cause dehydration and DKA or HHS. Hypoglycemia is glucose <70 mg/dL (<3.9 mmol/L), with <54 mg/dL (<3.0 mmol/L) more clinically significant.

### 17. Can diabetes go into remission?
**Answer:** Some people with type 2 diabetes achieve remission—usually A1C below the diabetes range for at least 3 months without glucose-lowering medicines—after weight loss, metabolic surgery or other interventions. Remission is not a cure; continued monitoring is needed.

### 18. Can people with diabetes live healthy, active lives?
**Answer:** Yes. Regular monitoring, appropriate food, physical activity, adequate sleep, prescribed treatment, preventive screening, vaccinations and emotional support can reduce risk and protect quality of life. Diabetes care is a long-term process, not a test of perfection.

### 19. What should I know about cardiovascular risk?
**Answer:** Diabetes increases risk of heart disease and stroke, but risk can be reduced by managing glucose, blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking, kidney disease, weight, activity and medicines with proven cardiovascular benefit when indicated.

### 20. What is the first practical step after diagnosis?
**Answer:** Arrange diabetes education and a follow-up plan; learn your medicine and glucose-monitoring technique; know your targets and emergency contacts; obtain needed supplies; and schedule eye, kidney, foot, blood-pressure and lipid risk assessment as appropriate.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Sections 2–4, 7–9; IDF Global Clinical Practice Recommendations 2025; ADA/EASD consensus on person-centred type 2 diabetes care.

# 2. Insulin injection

**Learning focus:** Give insulin safely, consistently and comfortably; recognize when technique or storage may be causing unexplained highs or lows.

### 1. Why is insulin injected?
**Answer:** Insulin is a protein and would be digested if swallowed. Most routine insulin is delivered into the subcutaneous fat with a pen, syringe, patch pump or pump. The device and insulin type must match the prescription.

### 2. What should I check before injecting?
**Answer:** Confirm the name and type of insulin, dose, timing, expiration, appearance and device. Check glucose and meal plan if instructed. Never inject an unlabeled product, a wrong insulin or insulin that has been frozen or overheated.

### 3. Where can subcutaneous insulin be injected?
**Answer:** Common sites are abdomen, outer thigh, buttock and upper arm. Keep away from scars, bruises, wounds, infection, moles and the area immediately around the navel. Absorption differs by site, so use a consistent, planned rotation.

### 4. Why must injection sites be rotated?
**Answer:** Repeated injections in one spot can cause lipohypertrophy—thick, lumpy tissue—or lipoatrophy. These areas may absorb insulin unpredictably and contribute to unexplained highs or lows. Rotate within a body area systematically, spacing injections.

### 5. How do I identify lipohypertrophy?
**Answer:** Look and feel for soft or firm thickened, raised or rubbery areas, tenderness or a difference from surrounding skin. Do not inject there. Ask a clinician or educator to examine sites and review technique, especially when glucose becomes unpredictable.

### 6. What needle length is usually preferred?
**Answer:** ADA 2026 and injection-technique recommendations support a 4-mm pen needle for many people, inserted at 90 degrees, to reduce intramuscular delivery. Technique may differ for syringes, children or special body types; follow an observed demonstration.

### 7. Can insulin be injected into muscle?
**Answer:** Routine intramuscular injection is not recommended because absorption can be faster and unpredictable, causing hypoglycemia. Use the prescribed needle and angle, avoid injecting into exercised limbs when possible, and ask for a technique review if very lean or unsure.

### 8. What is a safe pen-injection sequence?
**Answer:** Wash hands; check insulin and dose; attach a new needle; prime as directed; select the dose; place the needle into healthy subcutaneous tissue; press fully; keep the needle in place for the device-recommended count; remove and discard the needle safely.

### 9. Should I reuse pen needles or syringes?
**Answer:** Single-use needles are safest. Reuse blunts the needle, damages skin, increases tissue trauma and can cause dose inaccuracies or contamination. Never share pens, cartridges, syringes or needles, even if the needle is changed.

### 10. How should used sharps be discarded?
**Answer:** Place needles immediately into a puncture-resistant sharps container; do not recap, bend or break them. Follow local disposal rules. Keep sharps away from children and visitors. A diabetes clinic or pharmacy can advise on approved containers in your area.

### 11. How should unopened insulin usually be stored?
**Answer:** Most unopened insulin is refrigerated at 2–8°C, protected from light and never frozen. Do not use insulin that has frozen, overheated or passed its labeled expiry. Always follow the specific product leaflet because in-use storage differs by product.

### 12. How should an in-use pen or vial be stored?
**Answer:** Follow the manufacturer’s time and temperature instructions. Many products may be kept at room temperature for a limited period, which can improve comfort, but exposure to heat, sunlight or freezing can damage insulin. Record the opening date.

### 13. Can cloudy insulin be used?
**Answer:** Some suspensions, such as NPH, are meant to look uniformly cloudy after gentle rolling. Clear insulins should remain clear. Do not use insulin with unexpected particles, clumps, frosting or discoloration; ask the pharmacist if uncertain.

### 14. Can two insulins be mixed in one syringe?
**Answer:** Only if specifically prescribed and taught. Some insulins must never be mixed, including glargine or degludec with other insulins. When mixing is prescribed, use the exact sequence and monitoring plan taught by the diabetes team.

### 15. Should basal insulin be stopped if I am not eating?
**Answer:** People with type 1 diabetes generally must not stop basal insulin, even when unable to eat, because insulin omission can cause DKA. People with type 2 diabetes also need an individualized sick-day plan. Call the diabetes team rather than guessing.

### 16. What if I miss an insulin dose?
**Answer:** Do not automatically double the next dose. Check glucose and consider the insulin type, timing, food, ketones and risk of hypoglycemia. Use your written missed-dose plan or contact the diabetes team promptly; urgent assessment may be needed in type 1 diabetes.

### 17. What if I inject the wrong insulin or too much?
**Answer:** Check glucose more often, have fast-acting carbohydrate available if safe, avoid driving and contact your clinician or poison/emergency service for product-specific advice. A large or wrong dose can cause delayed hypoglycemia, so do not rely on feeling well initially.

### 18. Can exercise change absorption from an injection site?
**Answer:** Yes. Exercise involving an injected limb can increase blood flow and speed absorption. This may raise hypoglycemia risk. Discuss site selection, carbohydrate and insulin adjustments before new or prolonged exercise; do not change doses without a plan.

### 19. Does insulin injection cause dependence or mean I have failed?
**Answer:** No. Insulin is a replacement hormone or a treatment tool, not a punishment. Type 1 diabetes requires it; type 2 diabetes may need it as insulin production changes or during illness, pregnancy, surgery or severe hyperglycemia.

### 20. How often should injection technique be reviewed?
**Answer:** At diagnosis, when a device or insulin changes, when glucose becomes erratic, when skin changes occur and periodically during follow-up. Demonstrate the technique rather than only describing it; education should include site inspection, storage and sharps safety.

### 21. What is the difference between a spring-loaded pen like NovoRapid FlexTouch and a manual pen like NovoRapid FlexPen?
**Answer:** FlexTouch has a smooth, contoured barrel and uses a spring-loaded, motor-assisted mechanism that delivers the dose with very low effort; its push button does not extend outward as you dial the dose. FlexPen looks more like a traditional dial pen—its push button visibly extends outward proportionally to the dialed dose and requires active thumb pressure to inject.

### 22. Why might the maximum dose per injection differ between pen devices?
**Answer:** Device design limits how many units can be dialed in a single injection—for example, some spring-loaded pens allow up to 80 units per injection while some manual pens allow up to 60 units. If a prescribed dose is close to or above a device's maximum, confirm with a pharmacist or care team which pen is appropriate.

### 23. Does it matter if my insulin pen model has been discontinued and replaced with a different one?
**Answer:** Yes. If a prescribed device, such as a FlexTouch-style pen, is discontinued and only a different model, such as a FlexPen-style pen, is available, ask a pharmacist or diabetes educator to re-demonstrate the injection technique, since dialing motion, push-button behavior and audible feedback can differ between devices.

### 24. Why do some pens click while dialing and others only click at the end of the injection?
**Answer:** Manual pens often click as the dose is dialed and again while the dose is pushed in, while some spring-loaded pens give a single click only when the full dose has finished delivering. Learn a specific device's feedback pattern from a demonstration rather than assuming it matches a pen used before.

### 25. What is the difference between the Lantus SoloStar pen and the Sanofi AllStar pen device?
**Answer:** SoloStar is a prefilled, disposable pen that comes loaded with 300 units (3 mL) of insulin glargine; once empty, the entire pen is discarded in a sharps container. AllStar is a reusable pen body loaded with separate 3 mL Lantus cartridges—only the empty cartridge is replaced and discarded, while the pen device itself can typically be kept for up to about 3 years.

### 26. Does the maximum single dose differ between SoloStar and AllStar?
**Answer:** Yes. SoloStar allows up to 80 units in a single injection, while AllStar allows up to 60 units per injection; both dial in 1-unit steps. If a prescribed dose is close to a device's maximum, confirm the correct pen with a pharmacist.

### 27. Which pen type produces less plastic waste?
**Answer:** A reusable pen such as AllStar is more eco-friendly because only the small glass cartridge is discarded when insulin runs out, whereas a fully disposable pen such as SoloStar means the entire pen body is thrown away once empty.

### 28. Why might a reusable cartridge pen be a less convenient choice for travel?
**Answer:** A reusable pen requires carrying and correctly loading a separate insulin cartridge, and the pen mechanism itself must be cared for over time, whereas a prefilled disposable pen needs no loading and can simply be replaced with a new one—an important consideration when planning travel supplies.

### 29. What is a Lilly KwikPen, and how does it compare with FlexPen, FlexTouch or SoloStar?
**Answer:** KwikPen is Eli Lilly's line of prefilled, disposable insulin pens (for example Humalog, Humulin and Lyumjev)—similar in purpose to Novo Nordisk's FlexPen/FlexTouch and Sanofi's SoloStar. Each manufacturer's pen family has its own hardware and is not interchangeable between insulins, even though most dial in 1-unit steps.

### 30. Why is confusing a Humalog KwikPen U-100 with a U-200 pen dangerous?
**Answer:** U-200 is twice as concentrated as U-100 (200 units per mL versus 100 units per mL). Both pens still dial in units, but the U-200 pen delivers the same number of units in half the liquid volume. Using the wrong strength pen, or drawing U-200 insulin into a U-100 syringe, can cause a serious dosing error.

### 31. Why does the Humalog Junior KwikPen use half-unit dose increments?
**Answer:** It is designed for children and others who need very precise, small doses. Dialing in half-unit steps, up to a maximum of 30 units per injection, reduces the risk of accidental overdose in dose-sensitive patients.

### 32. What is Lyumjev, and how is it different from standard Humalog?
**Answer:** Lyumjev is an ultra-rapid-acting formulation of insulin lispro-aabc designed to start working faster than standard Humalog (insulin lispro)—similar in concept to how Fiasp is a faster formulation compared with standard NovoRapid.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Section 9; FITTER Forward expert recommendations (Mayo Clinic Proceedings 2025); ADA insulin-administration guidance; manufacturer product labeling (NovoRapid FlexTouch, NovoRapid FlexPen, Lantus SoloStar, Sanofi AllStar, Lilly KwikPen, Lyumjev).

# 3. Nutrition and diabetes

**Learning focus:** Build a culturally appropriate eating pattern that supports glucose, cardiovascular, kidney and weight goals without unnecessary restriction.

### 1. Is there one “diabetes diet”?
**Answer:** No. ADA and IDF support individualized eating patterns that fit culture, preferences, budget, medicines, health goals and food access. Mediterranean-style, plant-forward, lower-carbohydrate, vegetarian and other patterns can work when nutritionally adequate.

### 2. Are carbohydrates forbidden?
**Answer:** No. Carbohydrate-containing foods can be part of a healthy plan. The amount, quality, timing and insulin/medicine match matter. Prefer high-fiber sources such as vegetables, pulses, whole grains and whole fruit over sugary drinks and refined sweets.

### 3. What is the plate method?
**Answer:** A practical starting point is half the plate non-starchy vegetables, one quarter protein and one quarter high-fiber carbohydrate, with water or an unsweetened drink. Portions and composition should be adjusted for glucose, kidney health and appetite.

### 4. What is carbohydrate counting?
**Answer:** It is estimating the grams of carbohydrate in a meal so an insulin-to-carbohydrate plan can be used when prescribed. A dietitian or educator can teach label reading, household measures and local foods such as rice, roti, dal, fruit and snacks.

### 5. Should meals be skipped to lower glucose?
**Answer:** Skipping meals can cause hypoglycemia when using insulin or sulfonylureas, promote overeating later and worsen nutrition. Meal timing should match your medicines and daily routine. If appetite is poor, use the sick-day plan and seek advice.

### 6. Are whole fruits allowed?
**Answer:** Usually yes. Whole fruit provides fiber and nutrients; portion size and total carbohydrate matter. Juice and smoothies are easier to consume quickly and may raise glucose more rapidly. Pairing fruit with a balanced meal may help some people.

### 7. Are fruit juices and sugary drinks recommended?
**Answer:** They are generally not recommended as routine beverages because liquid sugar raises glucose quickly and provides little satiety. Water is preferred. Regular juice or glucose-containing drinks are useful for treating hypoglycemia, not for everyday hydration.

### 8. How does fiber help?
**Answer:** Fiber supports fullness, bowel health and cardiometabolic health; soluble fiber can slow glucose absorption. Increase vegetables, pulses, whole grains, nuts and seeds gradually with adequate fluids. Kidney disease may require individualized potassium, phosphorus or fiber advice.

### 9. What protein is appropriate?
**Answer:** Choose a pattern that includes pulses, beans, fish, eggs, unsweetened dairy, tofu, nuts or lean meats as appropriate. Kidney disease, pregnancy and personal preferences affect protein needs. Avoid assuming that very high-protein diets are safe for everyone.

### 10. How much fat should I eat?
**Answer:** Focus on fat quality: nuts, seeds, fish, avocado and unsaturated plant oils are generally preferable to trans fats and excess saturated fat. High-fat meals can delay and prolong glucose rise, which matters for insulin timing and monitoring.

### 11. What should I know about salt?
**Answer:** Excess sodium can worsen blood pressure and fluid retention. Limit highly processed foods, salty snacks and added salt; choose fresh foods and herbs where possible. People with kidney, heart or liver disease need individualized sodium guidance.

### 12. Can I use non-nutritive sweeteners?
**Answer:** They may help reduce sugar and calories for some people when used in moderation, but they do not make an overall diet healthy. Water, unsweetened tea and coffee are good default choices. Avoid replacing sugar with large amounts of refined starch.

### 13. Is weight loss always the goal?
**Answer:** Not for everyone. For people with overweight or obesity, modest, sustained weight loss can improve glucose and cardiometabolic health; for others, weight maintenance, strength, nutrition adequacy or preventing unintentional loss is more important. Goals must be individualized.

### 14. What should I know about alcohol?
**Answer:** Alcohol can cause delayed hypoglycemia, especially with insulin or sulfonylureas, and can impair recognition or treatment. Do not drink on an empty stomach, carry glucose, avoid driving and ask your clinician about personal limits and medicine interactions.

### 15. How can I adapt common Indian meals?
**Answer:** Keep the cultural meal, then adjust portions and balance: add non-starchy vegetables, dal or other protein; choose higher-fiber grains when suitable; limit sweet drinks and fried foods; and distribute carbohydrate across meals. A local dietitian can individualize this safely.

### 16. Does meal timing matter with insulin?
**Answer:** Yes. Rapid-acting insulin is usually linked to meals; basal insulin covers background needs. Timing depends on the product, glucose, meal certainty and clinician plan. Do not take a mealtime dose and then skip eating unless specifically instructed.

### 17. Can protein or fat cause a late glucose rise?
**Answer:** Large meals high in fat or protein may delay gastric emptying and produce a later rise in some people. People using pumps or intensive insulin should discuss pattern-based adjustments rather than making unsupervised extra doses.

### 18. What is a useful approach to food labels?
**Answer:** Check serving size, total carbohydrate, fiber, added sugars, saturated fat and sodium. Compare the portion you actually eat with the label serving. “Sugar-free” does not necessarily mean carbohydrate-free or low calorie.

### 19. What if food is expensive or unavailable?
**Answer:** Tell the care team; food insecurity is a health issue, not a personal failure. Affordable choices may include seasonal vegetables, pulses, eggs, unsweetened dairy and minimally processed staples. Social workers and community programs may help.

### 20. When should I see a dietitian?
**Answer:** At diagnosis, when insulin starts, during pregnancy, with kidney disease, recurrent hypoglycemia, weight change, disordered eating, food insecurity, sports training or persistent glucose patterns. Medical nutrition therapy should be practical, respectful and revisited as needs change.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Sections 5 and 9; IDF 2025; ADA/EASD consensus; relevant nutrition and kidney guidance from KDIGO.
# 4. Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar)

**Learning focus:** Recognize, treat and prevent low glucose, including episodes in which symptoms are absent or help is required.

### 1. What is hypoglycemia?
**Answer:** In diabetes, glucose <70 mg/dL (<3.9 mmol/L) is a warning value requiring action. Level 2 hypoglycemia is <54 mg/dL (<3.0 mmol/L). Level 3 is a severe event in which another person must provide assistance, regardless of the glucose number.

### 2. What symptoms can occur?
**Answer:** Shaking, sweating, hunger, palpitations, anxiety, tingling, dizziness, weakness, headache, irritability, blurred vision, confusion, unusual behavior, seizure or unconsciousness may occur. Symptoms differ between people and can be absent after repeated lows.

### 3. What commonly causes a low?
**Answer:** Too much insulin or a sulfonylurea, delayed or missed food, less carbohydrate than expected, unplanned activity, alcohol, kidney/liver disease, weight loss or recovery from illness can contribute. Often there is more than one cause.

### 4. How do I treat a conscious low?
**Answer:** Take 15 g of fast-acting glucose or carbohydrate, such as glucose tablets or measured juice. Recheck in 15 minutes; if still <70 mg/dL, repeat. Once recovered, eat a meal or a longer-acting snack if the next meal is not soon.

### 5. Why not use chocolate first?
**Answer:** Fat slows glucose absorption. Chocolate, high-fat sweets and protein foods are not ideal for the initial rescue. Use a measured glucose source first; choose a follow-up snack based on insulin activity, meal timing and the plan from your team.

### 6. What if the person is confused, unconscious or having a seizure?
**Answer:** Do not give food, drink or tablets by mouth. Place the person on their side, give prescribed glucagon if available and trained, call emergency services, and stay with them. Recovery after glucagon still requires urgent medical advice.

### 7. Who should have glucagon?
**Answer:** ADA recommends prescribing glucagon for everyone using insulin and for others at high risk of severe hypoglycemia. Family, friends, coworkers, school staff and caregivers should know where it is and how to use the available ready-to-use product.

### 8. Can a low recur after treatment?
**Answer:** Yes, especially after long-acting insulin, sulfonylureas, alcohol, kidney impairment or strenuous exercise. Continue monitoring and follow the individualized plan. Recurrent or unexplained lows require medication and meal review, not simply more snacks.

### 9. What is hypoglycemia unawareness?
**Answer:** It is reduced or absent warning symptoms before glucose becomes dangerously low. It raises risk of severe events. A period of avoiding lows, CGM with alerts, structured education and treatment adjustment may help restore awareness in some people.

### 10. Does exercise cause delayed hypoglycemia?
**Answer:** It can. Glucose may fall during activity or hours later, including overnight, especially after prolonged or intense exercise and with insulin. Discuss pre-, during- and post-exercise monitoring, carbohydrate and insulin strategies.

### 11. How does alcohol affect risk?
**Answer:** Alcohol can block the liver’s release of glucose and mask symptoms. Risk may occur many hours later, particularly without food or with insulin/sulfonylureas. Avoid alcohol during or after a low and ask for individualized advice.

### 12. Should I drive when my glucose is low?
**Answer:** No. Stop safely, treat the low and do not drive until glucose has recovered and you are mentally and physically able. Check before driving and during long journeys according to local advice; never drive if symptoms impair judgment.

### 13. Can CGM prevent severe hypoglycemia?
**Answer:** CGM with alerts can identify falling glucose and support prevention, especially in people using insulin or at high risk. It does not replace symptom awareness, a confirmatory meter when readings do not match how you feel, or a rescue plan.

### 14. What should be written in a hypoglycemia plan?
**Answer:** Include personal low threshold, symptoms, rescue carbohydrate amount, recheck timing, when to use glucagon, emergency contacts, medication instructions, exercise plan and driving/school/work steps. Share it with people who may need to help.

### 15. How can nighttime lows be recognized?
**Answer:** Sweating, damp bedding, nightmares, restless sleep, morning headache, fatigue or unexplained high glucose after a low may occur. CGM alerts can help. Recurrent overnight lows require review of basal insulin, evening food, alcohol and activity.

### 16. Can a person with diabetes treat someone else’s low?
**Answer:** Only if trained and if the treatment is safe. The person must be alert enough to swallow for oral carbohydrate. For severe hypoglycemia, use glucagon if available and call emergency services; never force food or drink into an unresponsive person.

### 17. Why can glucose be high after a low?
**Answer:** Counter-regulatory hormones raise glucose, and people may over-treat by eating much more than needed. Follow the measured rescue plan, then review the cause. Do not give a correction dose without considering active insulin and the clinician’s plan.

### 18. What medicines other than insulin can cause lows?
**Answer:** Sulfonylureas and meglitinides can cause hypoglycemia. Most other glucose-lowering medicines have low risk by themselves but can contribute when combined with insulin or secretagogues. Ask the pharmacist about every medicine, including herbal products.

### 19. When should recurrent lows prompt clinical review?
**Answer:** Immediately if severe, repeated, nocturnal, unexplained, associated with falls, driving, seizure, pregnancy, kidney disease or impaired awareness. The plan may need less intensive targets, different medicines, technology, education or support for food and medication access.

### 20. What should I carry every day?
**Answer:** Fast-acting glucose, meter or CGM supplies, prescribed medication, medical identification and emergency contact information. If prescribed, carry glucagon. Store supplies where they are accessible and tell trusted people how to help.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Section 6; ADA severe-hypoglycemia education; International Consensus on CGM metrics; Endocrine Society hypoglycemia guidance where applicable.

# 5. Monitoring diabetes

**Learning focus:** Use glucose and health measurements to make safer decisions with the clinical team—not to judge yourself.

### 1. What is self-monitoring of blood glucose (SMBG)?
**Answer:** SMBG uses a meter and finger-stick blood to measure glucose at a specific time. It is useful for insulin dosing when prescribed, identifying patterns, treating suspected lows and checking when CGM is unavailable or inaccurate.

### 2. What is continuous glucose monitoring (CGM)?
**Answer:** CGM measures glucose in interstitial fluid through a sensor and displays trends, alerts and data over time. It can help many people using insulin and selected people not using insulin, depending on access, preferences and clinical benefit.

### 3. When should I check glucose?
**Answer:** Timing depends on diabetes type, treatment and goals. Common times include fasting, before meals, 1–2 hours after meals when advised, before and after exercise, at bedtime, during illness and whenever symptoms do not match the reading.

### 4. What is time in range?
**Answer:** Time in range (TIR) is the percentage of CGM readings between 70 and 180 mg/dL (3.9–10.0 mmol/L). For many nonpregnant adults, a starting goal is >70% TIR, with time below 70 <4% and below 54 <1%, individualized safely.

### 5. What if CGM and symptoms disagree?
**Answer:** Interstitial glucose can lag behind blood glucose during rapid changes. If symptoms are severe, a reading is unexpected or treatment decisions are uncertain, confirm with a meter when possible and follow the device’s instructions for symptoms and calibration.

### 6. What is A1C monitoring frequency?
**Answer:** A1C is often checked at least twice yearly when stable and at goal, and about every 3 months when therapy changes or goals are not met. The exact interval depends on the clinician and whether the result is reliable.

### 7. What should I record besides glucose?
**Answer:** Record insulin/medicine timing, meals or carbohydrate estimates, activity, illness, stress, sleep, hypoglycemia, menstrual or pregnancy changes and device problems. Notes help identify patterns and prevent inappropriate changes based on one isolated result.

### 8. What is a glucose pattern?
**Answer:** A repeated relationship—such as fasting highs, after-meal spikes, activity-related lows or overnight falls—seen across several days. Pattern review is safer than reacting to every number. Look for causes and discuss changes with the care team.

### 9. How can I improve meter accuracy?
**Answer:** Wash and dry hands, use unexpired strips, store them correctly, apply enough blood, keep the meter clean and compare with a laboratory value when advised. Sugar or food on fingers can cause falsely high results.

### 10. How should I use CGM alerts?
**Answer:** Set alerts with your clinician so they are useful but not overwhelming. Keep alarms audible when safety matters, respond to falling trends early and have a backup treatment plan if the sensor fails or loses signal.

### 11. What routine health checks are important?
**Answer:** Review glucose and A1C, blood pressure, weight and medicines regularly. Depending on diabetes type and duration, assess lipids, kidney function and urine albumin, eyes, feet, dental health, vaccinations, cardiovascular risk, mental health and nutrition.

### 12. When is kidney screening done?
**Answer:** Many guidelines recommend urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio and estimated GFR at least annually for type 2 diabetes and for type 1 diabetes after the relevant duration, often beginning 5 years after diagnosis. Frequency changes with abnormal results or kidney disease.

### 13. When is an eye examination needed?
**Answer:** People with type 2 diabetes generally need a dilated or validated retinal examination at diagnosis; type 1 diabetes usually begins screening within 5 years of onset. Follow-up may be more frequent if retinopathy, pregnancy or other risk is present.

### 14. How often should feet be checked?
**Answer:** Inspect feet daily at home. A clinician should perform a comprehensive foot assessment at least annually, and more often with neuropathy, peripheral artery disease, deformity, prior ulcer, amputation or kidney failure. Risk-based frequency follows IWGDF guidance.

### 15. Why monitor blood pressure and lipids?
**Answer:** Blood pressure and cholesterol strongly affect heart, stroke, kidney and eye risk. Glucose control alone is not enough. Lifestyle and prescribed medicines—including statins or other therapies when indicated—should be reviewed with the clinical team.

### 16. What is a diabetes data review visit?
**Answer:** It is a collaborative review of glucose reports, medications, food, activity, hypoglycemia, psychosocial barriers, cost and goals. Bring meter, CGM or pump data and a list of all medicines. The aim is learning and safer action—not blame.

### 17. Why can A1C be misleading?
**Answer:** A1C may not reflect actual glucose when red-cell lifespan or hemoglobin is altered, such as with anemia, transfusion, hemoglobin variants, dialysis, pregnancy or some treatments. Compare with SMBG/CGM and use alternative markers when clinically appropriate.

### 18. Should I test ketones routinely?
**Answer:** Not everyone needs routine ketone tests. People at risk of DKA—especially type 1 diabetes, during illness, persistent high glucose, pregnancy or selected SGLT2 inhibitor use—should have a clinician-written plan for blood or urine ketone testing.

### 19. Can monitoring become harmful?
**Answer:** Excessive checking, alarm fatigue, fear or chasing every number can increase distress and unsafe dosing. Use the minimum monitoring that safely answers clinical questions, agree on targets and ask for support if monitoring is becoming overwhelming.

### 20. What is the goal of monitoring?
**Answer:** The goal is safe, informed action: prevent severe lows and DKA, understand patterns, guide treatment, protect organs, support pregnancy or activity and improve quality of life. A number is information, not a measure of personal worth.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Sections 4, 6, 7, 10–12; International Consensus on CGM; IDF 2025; KDIGO 2022; IWGDF 2023.

# 6. Diabetes and exercise

**Learning focus:** Make physical activity safer, practical and enjoyable while preventing hypoglycemia, dehydration and foot injury.

### 1. Is exercise safe with diabetes?
**Answer:** For most people, yes, and it improves glucose, fitness, mood, blood pressure and cardiovascular health. Start gradually and seek individualized advice if you have heart, kidney, eye, nerve or foot disease, pregnancy or severe hyperglycemia.

### 2. How much aerobic activity is usually recommended?
**Answer:** Many adults should work toward at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-to-vigorous activity on at least 3 days, with no more than 2 consecutive inactive days. Start with smaller amounts; any safe movement is better than none.

### 3. Is resistance training important?
**Answer:** Yes. Adults are generally advised to include resistance exercise 2–3 times weekly on nonconsecutive days, as appropriate. Strength training improves muscle function and insulin sensitivity. Begin with safe technique and progress gradually.

### 4. What about flexibility, balance and sitting?
**Answer:** Stretching and balance activities help mobility and fall prevention, especially in older adults. Break up sitting about every 30 minutes with light movement when possible. Adapt activity for neuropathy, vision problems or joint limitations.

### 5. How can activity lower glucose?
**Answer:** Contracting muscles take up glucose and improve insulin sensitivity. Glucose may fall during activity or later. Insulin, meal timing, activity intensity, duration, heat and fitness all affect the response, so learn from patterns with your team.

### 6. When should I check glucose around exercise?
**Answer:** If you use insulin or a medicine that can cause lows, check before and after unfamiliar or prolonged exercise and during it when advised. CGM trend arrows help but may lag. Carry rescue carbohydrate and know when to stop.

### 7. What if glucose is high before exercise?
**Answer:** Check for ketones if your plan advises, particularly with type 1 diabetes, illness or glucose around/above 250 mg/dL (13.9 mmol/L). Avoid vigorous exercise when ketones are elevated or you feel unwell; follow the sick-day plan and contact care.

### 8. What if glucose is low before activity?
**Answer:** Treat the low first and delay exercise until glucose is safe and stable. The amount of carbohydrate and whether insulin adjustment is needed depend on activity and your treatment. Do not exercise alone when severe lows are a concern.

### 9. Can exercise cause delayed nighttime lows?
**Answer:** Yes, particularly after prolonged or intense activity. Monitor later, consider a clinician-approved prevention plan and ensure someone knows how to respond. Pumps, basal insulin, meal and carbohydrate strategies may need individualized adjustment.

### 10. Should I change insulin for exercise?
**Answer:** Sometimes, but only using a plan developed with the diabetes team. Adjustments vary by insulin, activity, glucose trend, meal, duration, pregnancy and kidney function. Avoid making large unsupervised changes after a single workout.

### 11. How should I prevent foot problems during exercise?
**Answer:** Wear well-fitting, activity-appropriate footwear and moisture-managing socks; inspect feet before and after; never exercise barefoot if at risk; address blisters, redness or wounds promptly. People with neuropathy or prior ulcer need professional footwear advice.

### 12. What hydration is best?
**Answer:** Water is usually sufficient for moderate activity. Longer or intense exercise, heat or heavy sweating may require an individualized electrolyte plan. Avoid using sugary sports drinks routinely unless needed for activity or hypoglycemia management.

### 13. Is exercise different for type 1 and type 2 diabetes?
**Answer:** The benefits are similar, but type 1 diabetes requires more attention to insulin, ketones and delayed lows. Type 2 diabetes may involve medicines that affect glucose or hydration. Both require individualized planning rather than a one-size-fits-all rule.

### 14. Can I exercise with retinopathy?
**Answer:** Often yes, but proliferative or severe eye disease may require avoiding heavy straining, high-impact activity or activities that increase eye pressure until cleared. Ask the eye professional about safe intensity and warning symptoms.

### 15. Can I exercise with neuropathy or kidney disease?
**Answer:** Activity is usually beneficial but must be adapted. Choose low-impact options, protect feet, manage intensity and blood pressure, and seek guidance for advanced kidney disease, autonomic neuropathy, dizziness or high cardiovascular risk.

### 16. When is a pre-exercise medical assessment useful?
**Answer:** It is useful with symptoms of heart disease, known cardiovascular disease, long inactivity, advanced complications, high blood pressure, older age with multiple risks, pregnancy or plans for vigorous exercise. Routine testing is not needed for everyone.

### 17. Is exercise during pregnancy allowed?
**Answer:** Usually moderate activity such as walking, swimming or prenatal strength work is encouraged when no obstetric contraindication exists. Pregnancy, glucose targets, insulin and ketone risks require coordination with obstetric and diabetes teams.

### 18. What should I carry during activity?
**Answer:** Carry rapid carbohydrate, water, phone, identification, glucose meter/CGM supplies and prescribed medicines. For longer sessions, carry extra food and a plan for weather, location and assistance. Tell a partner or coach how to treat a low.

### 19. What are reasons to stop and seek help?
**Answer:** Stop for chest pain, severe breathlessness, fainting, confusion, severe weakness, suspected low, ketones, vomiting, unusual neurologic symptoms or a foot injury. Emergency symptoms require urgent medical care, not “pushing through.”

### 20. How can I make activity sustainable?
**Answer:** Choose enjoyable movement, schedule it, start small, use social support, vary intensity, track benefits beyond glucose and address pain or access barriers. A physiotherapist, exercise professional or diabetes educator can tailor a safe plan.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Section 5; ADA physical-activity recommendations; IDF physical activity guidance; IWGDF foot-prevention guidance.
# 7. Precautions during illness

**Learning focus:** Use a written sick-day plan to prevent dehydration, severe hypoglycemia and DKA/HHS.

### 1. Why does illness change glucose?
**Answer:** Infection, fever, pain, surgery, vomiting, stress and some medicines trigger hormones that can raise glucose and insulin needs. Poor intake, dehydration or kidney impairment can also cause lows. Illness can destabilize glucose even when usual routines are unchanged.

### 2. Should I stop insulin when I cannot eat?
**Answer:** Usually not, especially basal insulin in type 1 diabetes. Stopping insulin can cause DKA. The dose may need adjustment, but only according to a prearranged plan. Contact the diabetes team if you cannot eat, drink or keep medicines down.

### 3. How often should I check glucose while sick?
**Answer:** Many sick-day plans advise checking every 2–4 hours, including overnight when risk is high, but your plan may differ. Use CGM alerts and confirm with a meter when readings do not match symptoms or treatment decisions.

### 4. When should I check ketones?
**Answer:** Check when sick, vomiting, dehydrated, glucose is persistently high or your care plan specifies—often from about 240–250 mg/dL (13.3–13.9 mmol/L), and at lower levels in pregnancy or with SGLT2 inhibitors. Follow your personal thresholds.

### 5. What fluids are useful?
**Answer:** Sip water or sugar-free fluids regularly to prevent dehydration. If glucose is low or food intake is inadequate, use measured carbohydrate-containing fluids as instructed. Seek care if vomiting prevents hydration or urine becomes very reduced.

### 6. What should I eat if I cannot manage normal meals?
**Answer:** Use small, frequent portions of easy-to-tolerate carbohydrate and fluids according to your plan. Keep practical options available, such as soup, yogurt, crackers or measured liquids, while considering kidney, heart and pregnancy restrictions.

### 7. Can medicines need temporary changes during illness?
**Answer:** Yes. Dehydration, vomiting, fasting, kidney injury or surgery may require temporary changes to medicines such as SGLT2 inhibitors, metformin, diuretics or others. Do not apply a generic internet rule; obtain a clinician-written sick-day medication list.

### 8. What about SGLT2 inhibitors?
**Answer:** They can increase DKA risk, sometimes with only modest glucose elevation. During acute illness, fasting, dehydration or surgery, follow the prescriber’s hold/restart instructions and seek urgent care for nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, rapid breathing or ketones.

### 9. What if I have a fever or infection?
**Answer:** Check temperature, glucose and ketones as advised, drink fluids, use approved symptom medicines and contact the clinician for persistent fever, worsening symptoms or suspected infection. Some cold remedies contain sugar or ingredients that affect glucose or blood pressure.

### 10. What if I am vomiting or have diarrhea?
**Answer:** Contact the diabetes team early, particularly with type 1 diabetes, pregnancy, kidney disease or insulin use. Emergency assessment is needed for inability to keep fluids down, moderate/high ketones, breathing changes, confusion or severe dehydration.

### 11. What if glucose is low during illness?
**Answer:** Treat a conscious low with measured fast carbohydrate and recheck. If lows recur, contact the clinician because food intake, kidney function and medicines may have changed. Do not give oral food or drink to an unconscious person.

### 12. What if glucose is high but I feel okay?
**Answer:** Follow monitoring and correction instructions, hydrate and check ketones when indicated. People with type 1 diabetes can develop DKA before symptoms are dramatic. Persistent high glucose, ketones or worsening symptoms require prompt professional advice.

### 13. Which warning signs need emergency care?
**Answer:** Trouble breathing, deep or rapid breathing, fruity breath, severe abdominal pain, repeated vomiting, confusion, fainting, seizure, inability to drink, severe dehydration, moderate/high ketones, or glucose that remains very high despite the plan.

### 14. Should I take extra insulin during illness?
**Answer:** Illness may increase insulin needs, but extra doses should follow the written correction/ketone plan. Too much insulin can cause severe hypoglycemia. If no plan exists, contact the diabetes team or emergency service rather than guessing.

### 15. How can I prepare before illness occurs?
**Answer:** Keep extra insulin and medicines, meter/CGM supplies, ketone strips, thermometer, glucagon, fluids, easy foods, sick-day instructions and contact numbers. Check expiry dates and make sure family members know the emergency plan.

### 16. What should caregivers monitor?
**Answer:** Mental status, breathing, hydration, glucose trend, ketones, temperature, urine output, medication doses and ability to drink. Record results and times. Arrange help early if the person is alone, frail or unable to self-manage.

### 17. Can steroids raise glucose?
**Answer:** Yes. Steroids may cause marked or delayed hyperglycemia. Do not stop a prescribed steroid abruptly; contact the diabetes team about extra monitoring and temporary treatment. Ask before using steroid injections or tablets for other conditions.

### 18. Are vaccines important for people with diabetes?
**Answer:** Vaccination reduces the risk of infections that can destabilize diabetes. Follow national schedules for influenza, COVID-19, pneumococcal, hepatitis B and other vaccines recommended by age, risk and location.

### 19. When can I return to normal activity and medicines?
**Answer:** When hydration, food intake, symptoms, glucose and ketones have stabilized, and the clinician’s plan permits. Some medicines are restarted only after kidney function and eating/drinking are safe. Confirm rather than assuming.

### 20. What should every person with diabetes have in writing?
**Answer:** Target glucose range, testing frequency, ketone thresholds, insulin and medicine instructions, carbohydrate/fluid plan, correction steps, emergency signs, contacts, sick-leave advice and restart instructions. Review the plan at least annually and after treatment changes.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Section 6; ADA sick-day guidance; ADA/EASD/JBDS/AACE/DTS hyperglycemic-crisis consensus 2024; CDC sick-day guidance.

# 8. Diabetic ketoacidosis

**Learning focus:** Recognize DKA early, understand why it occurs and know when home monitoring is no longer safe.

### 1. What is DKA?
**Answer:** DKA is a life-threatening state caused by too little effective insulin, causing glucose elevation or diabetes history, ketone accumulation and metabolic acidosis. It can develop quickly and requires urgent medical assessment and treatment.

### 2. Why do ketones form?
**Answer:** When cells cannot access enough insulin-mediated glucose, the body breaks down fat for energy. The liver produces ketones. Excess ketones make the blood acidic; dehydration and electrolyte shifts then worsen the illness.

### 3. Who can develop DKA?
**Answer:** It is most common in type 1 diabetes but can occur in type 2 diabetes, new diabetes, pregnancy and people taking SGLT2 inhibitors. Insulin omission, infection, vomiting, fasting, trauma, surgery and pump failure are common triggers.

### 4. What are early symptoms?
**Answer:** Thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, high glucose, nausea, abdominal pain, reduced appetite and rising ketones may occur. Symptoms can resemble gastroenteritis. In type 1 diabetes, check glucose and ketones rather than waiting for severe symptoms.

### 5. What are emergency symptoms?
**Answer:** Repeated vomiting, deep or rapid breathing, fruity-smelling breath, severe abdominal pain, dehydration, confusion, drowsiness or collapse are emergencies. Call emergency services or go to the emergency department; do not attempt to manage severe symptoms at home.

### 6. What are the current diagnostic components of adult DKA?
**Answer:** The 2024 international consensus describes three components: diabetes or glucose ≥200 mg/dL (11.1 mmol/L) or known diabetes; β-hydroxybutyrate ≥3.0 mmol/L or urine ketones at least 2+; and pH <7.30 and/or bicarbonate <18 mmol/L. Medical diagnosis requires laboratory assessment.

### 7. Can DKA occur with normal or only mildly high glucose?
**Answer:** Yes. Euglycemic DKA can occur with SGLT2 inhibitors, pregnancy, fasting, reduced carbohydrate intake, vomiting or partial insulin treatment. Do not dismiss DKA because the glucose is not very high when symptoms or ketones are present.

### 8. What is the preferred ketone test?
**Answer:** Quantitative blood β-hydroxybutyrate is preferred when available because it measures the main ketone in DKA and tracks resolution. Urine ketones are useful when blood testing is unavailable but can lag early and remain positive during recovery.

### 9. When should I check ketones at home?
**Answer:** Follow the individualized plan; common triggers include illness, vomiting, persistent glucose around/above 240–250 mg/dL, pump interruption, pregnancy or SGLT2 inhibitor use. Check sooner if symptoms occur, even when glucose is not high.

### 10. What does a mildly raised ketone mean?
**Answer:** A mildly raised result may occur with fasting or illness and can progress. Hydrate if able, follow the prescribed insulin/ketone plan, recheck at the advised interval and contact the diabetes team. Never exercise vigorously with ketones.

### 11. What does a high ketone mean?
**Answer:** High or rising ketones, especially around ≥3.0 mmol/L blood β-hydroxybutyrate or urine ketones ≥2+, require urgent medical evaluation because they may meet DKA criteria. Do not wait for the glucose to rise further.

### 12. Does drinking water cure DKA?
**Answer:** No. Fluids may help dehydration but cannot replace insulin, electrolyte assessment and medical monitoring. DKA treatment commonly requires supervised fluids, insulin and potassium/electrolyte management, with treatment of the trigger.

### 13. Why is insulin essential in DKA treatment?
**Answer:** Insulin stops fat breakdown and ketone production while lowering glucose. In hospital it is given under a protocol with close glucose, ketone, electrolyte and acid–base monitoring. Insulin must not be stopped simply because glucose improves if ketones persist.

### 14. Why are electrolytes important?
**Answer:** DKA causes major total-body losses of water and electrolytes, especially potassium. Insulin shifts potassium into cells, so clinicians check potassium and kidney function and replace electrolytes safely. Do not self-treat with potassium.

### 15. What is the difference between DKA and HHS?
**Answer:** HHS is characterized by extreme hyperglycemia, hyperosmolarity and dehydration with little or no significant ketoacidosis; DKA has significant ketones and acidosis. They can overlap, and both are medical emergencies.

### 16. Can pump failure cause DKA quickly?
**Answer:** Yes. Rapid-acting insulin pump therapy has little or no long-acting insulin “backup.” A blocked cannula, empty reservoir, dislodged set or device failure can lead to ketones within hours. Follow the backup injection and contact plan.

### 17. How can I prevent recurrent DKA?
**Answer:** Ensure affordable insulin and supplies, never intentionally omit basal insulin, use sick-day and ketone plans, check pump sites, address mental-health or eating concerns, arrange education after an admission and schedule close follow-up.

### 18. Can cannabis or other substances matter?
**Answer:** Recurrent vomiting, dehydration or difficulty managing insulin can increase DKA risk. Discuss cannabis, alcohol and other substances without judgment. Seek care for vomiting and ketones, and do not assume symptoms are only intoxication or stomach illness.

### 19. Is DKA in pregnancy different?
**Answer:** Pregnancy can accelerate ketosis and DKA may occur at lower glucose levels. Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, breathing changes or ketones require immediate obstetric and diabetes assessment. Do not wait for severe hyperglycemia.

### 20. What should happen after DKA recovery?
**Answer:** Before discharge, review the trigger, insulin technique, access, ketone testing, sick-day rules, pump backup, nutrition, mental health and follow-up. A structured prevention plan and early review reduce recurrence. Never regard an admission as a personal failure.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Section 6; ADA/EASD/JBDS/AACE/DTS Consensus Report, Diabetes Care 2024; ISPAD DKA guideline 2022 for children/adolescents.

# 9. Long-term complications of diabetes

**Learning focus:** Prevent, detect and treat blood-vessel, nerve, eye, kidney, foot, oral and cardiovascular complications early.

### 1. What are diabetes complications?
**Answer:** Persistent glucose elevation and associated blood-pressure, lipid and inflammatory risks can damage small vessels (eyes, kidneys and nerves) and large vessels (heart, brain and legs). Complications are not inevitable and risk can be reduced.

### 2. What is diabetic retinopathy?
**Answer:** Retinopathy is damage to retinal blood vessels. It may have no symptoms until advanced. Regular retinal screening, glucose and blood-pressure management, lipid treatment and timely ophthalmic therapy help protect vision.

### 3. What symptoms require urgent eye care?
**Answer:** Sudden vision loss, new floaters, flashes, a curtain-like shadow, eye pain or rapidly worsening blur require urgent assessment. Do not wait for the next routine appointment. Pregnancy can increase retinopathy risk and needs planned screening.

### 4. What is diabetic kidney disease?
**Answer:** It is kidney damage associated with diabetes, often detected by raised urine albumin or reduced eGFR. Early disease may have no symptoms. Blood pressure, glucose, kidney-protective medicines when indicated and avoidance of kidney injury are important.

### 5. How are kidneys screened?
**Answer:** Screening uses urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio and eGFR blood testing, often annually or more frequently when abnormal. Results can vary with infection, exercise, menstruation or acute illness; persistent abnormalities need clinical confirmation.

### 6. What is diabetic neuropathy?
**Answer:** Neuropathy is nerve dysfunction that may cause burning, tingling, numbness, pain, weakness, altered balance or loss of protective sensation. It can also affect digestion, blood pressure, sweating, bladder and sexual function.

### 7. Why is foot care important?
**Answer:** Numbness, reduced blood flow, deformity and pressure can allow a small injury to become an ulcer or infection. Daily inspection, suitable footwear, professional risk assessment and prompt treatment reduce risk of serious foot disease.

### 8. What should I look for on my feet?
**Answer:** Check for redness, warmth, swelling, cuts, blisters, cracks, drainage, color change, callus, nail problems or new deformity. Use a mirror or ask for help. Do not walk barefoot when at risk.

### 9. How should a foot wound be managed?
**Answer:** Do not self-treat with harsh chemicals, corn plasters or untrained cutting. Clean and cover as instructed, reduce pressure and contact the diabetes/foot team promptly. Spreading redness, warmth, swelling, pus, fever or black tissue is urgent.

### 10. Can diabetes affect the heart and brain?
**Answer:** Yes. Diabetes increases risk of coronary disease, heart failure and stroke. Manage glucose, blood pressure, cholesterol, smoking, kidney disease, activity and weight, and use cardiovascular-protective medicines when clinically indicated.

### 11. What are stroke warning signs?
**Answer:** Sudden facial droop, arm weakness, speech difficulty, vision loss, severe imbalance or confusion require emergency services immediately. Note the time symptoms began. Do not wait for glucose to normalize before seeking help.

### 12. How does blood pressure affect complications?
**Answer:** High blood pressure accelerates kidney, eye, heart and brain damage. Home readings can help but do not replace validated clinic assessment. The target and treatment depend on cardiovascular risk, kidney disease, age and tolerability.

### 13. How does cholesterol matter?
**Answer:** LDL and other atherogenic lipoproteins contribute to vascular disease. Statins or other lipid-lowering treatment may be indicated based on age, risk and existing disease. Discuss muscle symptoms, pregnancy plans and adherence with the clinician.

### 14. Does smoking matter if glucose is controlled?
**Answer:** Yes. Smoking and tobacco increase cardiovascular, kidney, eye and foot risk. Quitting is one of the most effective risk-reduction steps. Ask about counseling, nicotine replacement and medicines; support is more effective than blame.

### 15. Can diabetes affect teeth and gums?
**Answer:** Diabetes may increase gum disease, dry mouth, infections and delayed healing. Brush with fluoride toothpaste, clean between teeth, avoid tobacco and attend dental care. Persistent bleeding, swelling, pain or loose teeth need review.

### 16. Can diabetes affect sexual or bladder health?
**Answer:** Nerve, vessel, hormone, medication and psychological factors may contribute to erectile, arousal, lubrication, ejaculation or bladder problems. These are common and treatable. Discuss them privately with a clinician; do not buy unregulated medicines online.

### 17. Can complications be prevented if I feel well?
**Answer:** Yes, and screening remains essential because early disease is often silent. Good glucose, blood pressure and lipid management, smoking cessation, activity, vaccination, kidney protection, dental and foot care all contribute.

### 18. How does pregnancy affect complications?
**Answer:** Pregnancy can worsen pre-existing retinopathy or kidney disease, particularly with rapid glucose improvement or uncontrolled risk factors. Preconception eye, kidney, blood-pressure and medication review is important, with pregnancy-specific follow-up.

### 19. How can kidney disease change diabetes treatment?
**Answer:** Reduced kidney function may increase insulin or medicine exposure and hypoglycemia risk; some medicines need dose adjustment or avoidance. Nutrition, blood pressure and kidney-protective therapy must be coordinated with the diabetes and kidney teams.

### 20. What is the most important prevention message?
**Answer:** Attend scheduled screening even without symptoms, take prescribed risk-reduction treatment, inspect feet and eyes, report warning signs early, and address barriers to care. Complication prevention is a team effort and can be improved at every visit.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, Sections 6, 10–12; IDF 2025; KDIGO 2022; IWGDF 2023; ophthalmology, cardiovascular and kidney specialty guidance.
# 10. Diabetes and your sentiments

**Learning focus:** Recognize diabetes distress and mental-health concerns, use support and seek help early without shame.

### 1. What is diabetes distress?
**Answer:** Diabetes distress is the emotional burden of constant self-care, numbers, injections, fears, costs, family expectations or future complications. It is common and understandable; it is not laziness and it is different from clinical depression.

### 2. What is diabetes burnout?
**Answer:** Burnout is feeling exhausted, hopeless or unable to continue diabetes tasks. It may show as missed doses, avoiding monitoring or “giving up.” Support, simplification, practical problem-solving and mental-health care can help; criticism usually does not.

### 3. How is depression different from distress?
**Answer:** Depression involves persistent low mood or loss of interest plus symptoms such as sleep/appetite change, low energy, guilt, poor concentration or thoughts of death. It affects life broadly and needs professional assessment; distress may focus mainly on diabetes.

### 4. What is anxiety in diabetes?
**Answer:** Anxiety may involve excessive worry about lows, complications, injections, pregnancy, work or judgment. Panic, avoidance or sleep disruption can impair care. Anxiety is treatable through education, psychological therapies, medication when indicated and a safer care plan.

### 5. Should emotional health be screened?
**Answer:** Yes. ADA recommends periodic assessment for diabetes distress, depression, anxiety, disordered eating and cognitive or social barriers, especially when treatment goals are not met, complications occur or life circumstances change.

### 6. What can I do if I feel overwhelmed?
**Answer:** Tell a trusted person and the diabetes team; choose one urgent practical problem; simplify tasks where safe; schedule follow-up; use DSMES; and request mental-health support. Small, consistent steps are more useful than trying to change everything at once.

### 7. How can family help?
**Answer:** Ask what support is wanted, learn low treatment, glucagon and sick-day steps, avoid policing food or numbers, and praise problem-solving. Family meetings can reduce conflict. Children and adolescents need age-appropriate shared responsibility rather than blame.

### 8. What if I fear hypoglycemia?
**Answer:** Fear can cause under-dosing insulin, overeating or avoiding activity. Share the fear; review actual episodes, targets, CGM alerts, glucagon, driving and exercise plans. Safer treatment and structured education can reduce fear without sacrificing necessary care.

### 9. Can stress raise glucose?
**Answer:** Stress hormones, sleep loss, changes in eating and reduced activity can raise glucose; some people experience different responses. Stress management does not replace medication. Track patterns and use counseling, breathing, movement, social support or other strategies.

### 10. How does sleep affect diabetes?
**Answer:** Too little, irregular or poor-quality sleep can affect appetite, insulin sensitivity, mood and glucose. Aim for a regular routine and discuss snoring, witnessed apnea, insomnia or shift work. Sleep treatment may improve health and self-management.

### 11. What is disordered eating in diabetes?
**Answer:** It includes restriction, binge eating, purging, insulin omission for weight control, compulsive exercise or intense fear of food/weight. Insulin omission can cause DKA. Seek a team experienced in eating disorders and diabetes; do not respond with punishment.

### 12. Can diabetes affect relationships and intimacy?
**Answer:** Treatment burden, sexual symptoms, mood, fear of lows and differing food or activity preferences can strain relationships. Honest communication and professional support help. A partner can learn emergency care without becoming a “diabetes police officer.”

### 13. How can I handle stigma?
**Answer:** Diabetes is not a moral failure. Choose who receives information, correct myths briefly, use person-first or preferred language and request respectful care. Peer groups and diabetes organizations can reduce isolation and improve confidence.

### 14. What if cost or access is causing distress?
**Answer:** Tell the clinician directly. Ask about generic or biosimilar options, assistance programs, community resources, insurance navigation, social work and safer supply planning. Never ration insulin or reuse unsafe supplies without urgent professional guidance.

### 15. Can cognitive problems affect self-management?
**Answer:** Depression, sleep disorders, hypoglycemia, aging, illness and some complications can impair memory or planning. Use reminders, simplified regimens, pill/insulin organizers and a trusted caregiver, while preserving autonomy and reviewing safety.

### 16. How should children and adolescents be supported?
**Answer:** Use developmentally appropriate education, shared problem-solving, school plans and screening for distress, depression, anxiety and disordered eating. Gradually transfer tasks while adults retain safety oversight. Avoid using glucose or food as a measure of behavior or worth.

### 17. What if I have thoughts of self-harm?
**Answer:** Tell someone now, move away from medicines or means if safe, and contact emergency services or a crisis line in your country. Do not stay alone. The diabetes team should know because insulin access and safety planning require compassionate coordination.

### 18. Is asking for psychological help a weakness?
**Answer:** No. Mental health is part of diabetes care. Counseling, cognitive-behavioral therapy, family therapy, peer support and medication when appropriate can improve quality of life and self-management. Ask for a professional familiar with diabetes when possible.

### 19. What is a realistic self-care goal?
**Answer:** Choose a small behavior that matters to you—checking at an agreed time, taking basal insulin, walking after one meal, preparing rescue glucose or attending an appointment. Review what helped and revise without self-blame.

### 20. What should clinicians and families remember?
**Answer:** Ask “How are you coping?” as well as “What is your glucose?” Listen without judgment, screen when indicated, address social barriers, simplify safely and celebrate strengths. Emotional safety is a clinical safety issue.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, psychosocial and health-behavior sections; ADA Diabetes and Emotional Health resources; IDF person-centred education principles.

# 11. Some suggestions for travel

**Learning focus:** Plan for time zones, transport, food, temperature, devices, emergencies and access to care.

### 1. Should I speak with my diabetes team before travel?
**Answer:** Yes, especially for long-distance travel, remote destinations, pregnancy, recurrent hypoglycemia, pump/CGM use, recent DKA, kidney/heart disease or complex insulin schedules. Review a written plan, supplies, vaccines and travel insurance.

### 2. What supplies should I pack?
**Answer:** Carry more insulin, medicines, needles, strips, lancets, sensors, chargers, batteries, ketone tests, glucagon, fast carbohydrate, medical identification and prescriptions than the trip length requires. Keep a backup meter and supplies in separate bags.

### 3. Where should insulin and supplies travel?
**Answer:** Keep insulin, devices and essential medicines in carry-on baggage, not checked luggage, where freezing, heat, delay or loss can occur. Bring a clinician letter if useful and check airline/security rules before departure.

### 4. How should insulin be protected from temperature extremes?
**Answer:** Avoid direct sun, hot vehicles, freezing conditions and unapproved ice contact. Use a validated travel container according to product instructions. Check insulin before use; do not use it if frozen, overheated or visibly altered.

### 5. How do time zones affect insulin?
**Answer:** Crossing time zones changes meal and injection intervals. Obtain a written plan for basal insulin, mealtime insulin, pump settings and overnight monitoring before departure. Do not improvise by doubling or skipping doses.

### 6. What should I do during a long flight or journey?
**Answer:** Carry rescue carbohydrate and water, monitor more often if using insulin, move legs regularly, keep supplies accessible and use the agreed meal/insulin plan. Avoid alcohol if it increases risk. Never place insulin or CGM supplies in checked baggage.

### 7. Can travel food cause glucose changes?
**Answer:** Yes. Portions, carbohydrate, meal timing, dehydration and activity vary. Carry familiar snacks, read labels when possible, match insulin to the actual meal as instructed and monitor patterns. Do not assume “healthy” travel food is low in carbohydrate.

### 8. How should I handle airport security with devices?
**Answer:** Check current manufacturer and airport guidance for pumps, CGMs, scanners and X-ray equipment. Request a hand inspection when the device instructions advise avoiding certain scanners. Do not remove a device unless trained and it is safe.

### 9. Should I carry a medical letter?
**Answer:** A letter can state the diagnosis, medicines, insulin, devices, need for supplies and emergency treatment. Carry prescriptions using generic names and copies digitally and on paper. Keep documents with—not inside—checked baggage.

### 10. What should medical identification include?
**Answer:** “Diabetes,” insulin use if relevant, pump/CGM, emergency contact and important allergies or conditions. A bracelet, necklace, wallet card or phone medical ID can help responders when you cannot speak.

### 11. What if I become hypoglycemic while traveling?
**Answer:** Stop the activity, test if possible, treat with measured fast carbohydrate, recheck and do not drive until recovered. Tell a travel companion how to use glucagon. Carry more rescue carbohydrate than you think you will need.

### 12. How can I prevent foot problems while sightseeing?
**Answer:** Wear broken-in, supportive footwear; never walk barefoot; inspect feet daily; keep feet clean and dry; avoid blisters and burns; and plan rest. Seek medical care quickly for redness, swelling, wound, drainage or fever.

### 13. What about vaccines and infection prevention?
**Answer:** Review routine and destination vaccines well before travel. Use safe food and water practices, hand hygiene, sun/heat protection and insect precautions. Illness can destabilize glucose, so carry the sick-day and ketone plan.

### 14. Should I buy insulin or medicines abroad?
**Answer:** Avoid relying on an unfamiliar supply. If replacement is needed, use a reputable regulated source and involve a clinician/pharmacist because concentrations, devices and brands differ. Never change insulin concentration or type without expert advice.

### 15. Can I travel with needles and sharps?
**Answer:** Usually yes with appropriate packaging and documentation, but rules vary by country and carrier. Keep needles in original packaging where possible and use a travel sharps container. Check destination regulations in advance.

### 16. What if my pump or CGM fails?
**Answer:** Carry backup injections, basal insulin if prescribed for emergencies, syringes/pen needles, written settings and a contact number. Follow the device-specific failure plan. Check glucose and ketones more often until reliable therapy is restored.

### 17. What if I develop vomiting or ketones abroad?
**Answer:** Follow the sick-day plan, hydrate if able, continue essential insulin as instructed, check glucose/ketones and seek urgent local medical care for high/rising ketones, breathing changes, confusion or inability to drink. Know the local emergency number.

### 18. How can I manage heat, altitude or unusual activity?
**Answer:** Heat, dehydration, altitude and increased walking can change glucose and insulin absorption. Monitor more often, hydrate, protect insulin and feet, carry carbohydrate and adjust only according to a clinician-approved plan.

### 19. Is travel insurance important?
**Answer:** Yes. Declare diabetes accurately, confirm coverage for medicines, devices, evacuation and pregnancy when relevant, and keep emergency contacts. Pre-existing-condition rules vary. Carry enough supplies because replacement may be difficult or expensive.

### 20. What is a simple travel checklist?
**Answer:** Medicines and extra supplies; carry-on storage; insulin temperature plan; glucose/ketone monitoring; rescue carbohydrate/glucagon; medical ID and letter; time-zone plan; food/water; footwear; vaccines; insurance; emergency contacts; and backup therapy.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA travel and self-management resources; CDC travel and diabetes guidance; ADA Standards of Care 2026; IDF education and access principles.

# 12. Diabetes and pregnancy

**Learning focus:** Plan pregnancy safely, manage glucose and medicines with a specialist team, and protect the health of the pregnant person and baby.

### 1. Can people with diabetes have a healthy pregnancy?
**Answer:** Yes. Preconception planning, glucose optimization, appropriate medicines, nutrition, activity, eye/kidney review and coordinated obstetric-diabetes care substantially improve safety. Pregnancy should be planned when possible, but unplanned pregnancy deserves prompt, nonjudgmental care.

### 2. What is preconception care?
**Answer:** It is care before pregnancy to review A1C and glucose, blood pressure, kidney and urine albumin, eyes, thyroid when indicated, medicines, folic acid, vaccines, weight, nutrition, contraception and pregnancy goals with the care team.

### 3. Why should glucose be improved before conception?
**Answer:** Early fetal organs develop before many people know they are pregnant. High glucose around conception increases risk of miscarriage and congenital anomalies. The safest target is individualized; many guidelines aim for A1C <6.5% before conception when achievable without problematic hypoglycemia.

### 4. What should I do if pregnancy occurs unexpectedly?
**Answer:** Contact the diabetes and obstetric teams promptly. Do not stop insulin or prescribed medicines suddenly. Review all medicines, begin/continue prenatal folic acid as advised, increase monitoring as directed and seek urgent care for vomiting, ketones or severe glucose changes.

### 5. Which glucose targets are commonly used in pregnancy?
**Answer:** Common ADA/ACOG targets are fasting/premeal 70–95 mg/dL (3.9–5.3), 1-hour after meal 110–140 mg/dL (6.1–7.8) or 2-hour 100–120 mg/dL (5.6–6.7), with an individualized A1C goal often <6% if safe. Local protocols may differ.

### 6. Why are lows more concerning during pregnancy?
**Answer:** Pregnancy targets are lower and insulin needs change, increasing hypoglycemia risk. Nausea, sleep and altered symptoms may make lows harder to recognize. Carry glucose, use a clinician-approved plan, consider CGM and ensure caregivers know glucagon.

### 7. Is insulin safe during pregnancy?
**Answer:** Insulin is widely used because it does not cross the placenta in the same way as many oral medicines and can be adjusted precisely. The specific insulin and regimen should be selected by the pregnancy-diabetes team.

### 8. Can non-insulin medicines be used?
**Answer:** Medication safety and recommendations differ by drug and guideline. Some cross the placenta or lack sufficient long-term safety data. Do not stop or continue tablets, injectables or SGLT2 inhibitors without obstetric and diabetes advice; insulin is commonly preferred when medication is required for GDM.

### 9. What is gestational diabetes mellitus (GDM)?
**Answer:** GDM is diabetes first recognized during pregnancy that is not clearly overt pre-existing diabetes. It often develops at 24–28 weeks as placental hormones increase insulin resistance, but early testing is needed for people at higher risk.

### 10. How is GDM managed initially?
**Answer:** Education, individualized nutrition, appropriate activity, glucose monitoring and weight-gain guidance are usual first steps. If targets are not reached, medication—often insulin—is added. Targets and treatment must be coordinated with the obstetric team.

### 11. How often should glucose be checked in pregnancy?
**Answer:** Many people check fasting and after meals, sometimes before meals, at bedtime or overnight, depending on treatment. Type 1 diabetes may require frequent SMBG or CGM. Use the schedule prescribed; do not chase numbers with unsupervised doses.

### 12. What if ketones develop during pregnancy?
**Answer:** Pregnancy can produce DKA at lower glucose levels. Check ketones when sick, vomiting, fasting or glucose is high as advised. Contact the diabetes/obstetric team urgently for ketones, nausea, abdominal pain, rapid breathing or inability to drink.

### 13. Is exercise recommended during pregnancy?
**Answer:** When there is no obstetric contraindication, moderate activity such as walking, swimming or prenatal strengthening is usually beneficial. Avoid dehydration, overheating, hypoglycemia and activities with fall/trauma risk. Follow individual obstetric advice.

### 14. What nutrition principles help during pregnancy?
**Answer:** Eat regular balanced meals/snacks, distribute carbohydrate, include vegetables, fiber and adequate protein, choose nutrient-dense foods and avoid sugary beverages. Do not use extreme carbohydrate restriction or weight-loss diets. A pregnancy dietitian can tailor cultural foods.

### 15. What monitoring is needed for pre-existing diabetes?
**Answer:** In addition to glucose, care may include eye examinations, kidney and blood-pressure assessment, thyroid testing when indicated, fetal growth and wellbeing monitoring, medication review and planning for labor, delivery and postpartum insulin changes.

### 16. Can retinopathy or kidney disease worsen in pregnancy?
**Answer:** It can, especially with pre-existing disease or rapid changes in glucose and blood pressure. Complete eye and kidney assessment before conception when possible and follow the recommended schedule during pregnancy.

### 17. What happens to insulin needs after delivery?
**Answer:** Placental hormone withdrawal can rapidly reduce insulin resistance. People with type 1 or type 2 diabetes often need prompt postpartum insulin adjustment to prevent lows; GDM treatment may stop, but this must be confirmed with the team.

### 18. Can I breastfeed with diabetes or insulin?
**Answer:** Usually yes, and breastfeeding has health benefits. It can increase glucose use and cause lows in people using insulin, so keep food, fluids and glucose available and review medication and monitoring with the team.

### 19. What follow-up is needed after GDM?
**Answer:** A 75-g oral glucose tolerance test is commonly recommended 4–12 weeks postpartum, followed by lifelong diabetes screening at least every 1–3 years depending on risk. Healthy eating, activity, weight goals, breastfeeding and future pregnancy planning matter.

### 20. What contraception and future-pregnancy advice is important?
**Answer:** Discuss contraception until glucose, medicines and health conditions are optimized for pregnancy. GDM or a previous diabetic pregnancy increases future type 2 and recurrent GDM risk. Preconception review should occur before every future pregnancy.

**Evidence anchors:** ADA Standards of Care 2026, pregnancy section; Endocrine Society/European Society of Endocrinology 2025 pre-existing diabetes in pregnancy guidance; ACOG Practice Bulletin 190 and updates; IDF GDM resources; ADA/EASD/JBDS/AACE/DTS 2024 consensus for DKA in pregnancy.

# 13. Types of insulin

**Learning focus:** Recognize the five action-profile categories of insulin, match common Indian brand names to their molecule and category, and understand why molecule, brand and concentration all matter for safety.

### 1. What are the main categories of insulin based on how they act in the body?
**Answer:** Insulins are generally grouped by onset, peak and duration: rapid-acting, short-acting (regular/human), intermediate-acting (NPH/isophane), long- and ultra-long-acting (basal), and premixed insulins that combine two action profiles in one product.

### 2. What is rapid-acting insulin and when is it used?
**Answer:** Rapid-acting insulin analogs (insulin aspart, lispro, glulisine) start working in about 15 minutes, peak around 1–1.5 hours and last 3–4 hours. They are usually given just before or with a meal to cover the after-meal glucose rise.

### 3. Which brand names are commonly used for rapid-acting insulin in India?
**Answer:** Insulin aspart is sold as NovoRapid and the ultra-rapid Fiasp (Novo Nordisk); insulin lispro as Humalog (Eli Lilly) and Eglucent (Lupin); insulin glulisine as Apidra (Sanofi). Always confirm the exact molecule and brand printed on your prescription, not just the box color or pen shape.

### 4. What is short-acting (regular/human) insulin?
**Answer:** Regular human insulin is unmodified human insulin usually injected about 30 minutes before a meal. It starts working in roughly 30 minutes, peaks at 2–3 hours and lasts 6–8 hours—slower to start and longer-acting than rapid-acting analogs.

### 5. Which Indian brands are regular human insulin?
**Answer:** Examples include Actrapid (Novo Nordisk), Huminsulin R (Eli Lilly), Insuman Rapid (Sanofi), Insugen-R (Biocon) and Wosulin R (Wockhardt). The "R" in these brand names commonly signals regular/rapid human insulin.

### 6. What is intermediate-acting (NPH) insulin?
**Answer:** NPH, or isophane insulin, is a cloudy suspension used for baseline (background) glucose control. It starts working in about 1.5–2 hours, peaks at 6–8 hours and lasts 12–16 hours, so it is often given once or twice daily.

### 7. Which Indian brands contain NPH insulin?
**Answer:** Examples include Insulatard (Novo Nordisk), Huminsulin N (Eli Lilly), Insuman Basal (Sanofi), Insugen-N (Biocon) and Wosulin N (Wockhardt). NPH must be gently rolled or tipped before use because it looks uniformly cloudy, unlike clear insulins.

### 8. What are long-acting and ultra-long-acting basal insulins?
**Answer:** These provide a steady, largely flat background insulin level for 18–24 hours or longer, without a strong peak, which lowers the risk of unpredictable lows compared with NPH. They include insulin glargine, detemir and degludec.

### 9. Which Indian brands are insulin glargine?
**Answer:** Lantus (Sanofi, U100 concentration), Toujeo (Sanofi, U300—a more concentrated, longer-lasting formulation), Basaglar (Eli Lilly/Boehringer Ingelheim), Basalog (Biocon) and Glaritus (Wockhardt) are glargine-based products.

### 10. What about insulin detemir and insulin degludec?
**Answer:** Insulin detemir is marketed as Levemir (Novo Nordisk). Insulin degludec, an ultra-long-acting insulin that can last up to about 42 hours, is marketed as Tresiba (Novo Nordisk).

### 11. Why does the concentration of insulin, such as U100 versus U300, matter?
**Answer:** Toujeo (U300) contains three times the insulin per mL compared with a standard U100 product. Units are still dosed as units, but absorption and duration differ, so products are not automatically interchangeable unit-for-unit—dose changes must be individualized by the care team.

### 12. What are premixed human insulins?
**Answer:** These combine regular and NPH insulin in a fixed ratio, commonly 30% regular/70% NPH, in one injection to cover both background and mealtime needs. Examples include Human Mixtard 30, Huminsulin 30/70, Insuman Comb 30, Insugen 30/70 and Wosulin 30/70; 50/50 ratios also exist.

### 13. What are premixed analog insulins?
**Answer:** These combine a rapid-acting analog with a protamine-crystallized, intermediate-acting form of the same analog. Examples include NovoMix 30 (30% aspart/70% protaminated aspart) and Humalog Mix 25 or Mix 50 (lispro/protamine-lispro blends).

### 14. What is Ryzodeg and how is it different from other premixes?
**Answer:** Ryzodeg combines 70% insulin degludec (ultra-long-acting) with 30% insulin aspart (rapid-acting) in one pen—pairing a peakless basal component with a mealtime component, unlike human premixes that use NPH as the intermediate component.

### 15. What is a "biosimilar" insulin, and does it matter which one I use?
**Answer:** A biosimilar is an insulin product developed to be highly similar to an already-approved insulin—for example, Basalog, Glaritus and Eglucent are biosimilar or generic versions of glargine- or lispro-type molecules. Never switch between brands or biosimilars on your own; your clinician decides if and how to change products safely.

### 16. Why do premixed insulins offer less flexibility than a basal-bolus plan?
**Answer:** A fixed premix locks the ratio between background and mealtime insulin into every dose, which is simpler to inject but cannot be adjusted separately for an unusual meal, exercise or illness. A basal-bolus plan uses separate long-acting and rapid-acting insulins, so each can be adjusted independently.

### 17. Can rapid-acting insulin be used alone as a full-day basal replacement?
**Answer:** No. Rapid-acting insulin acts for only 3–4 hours, so using it alone would leave gaps with no insulin cover, risking high glucose and ketone formation between doses. A separate long-acting or intermediate insulin is needed for background coverage.

### 18. Why do brand names sometimes cause confusion or dosing errors?
**Answer:** Many brand names look or sound alike—for example, Humalog (a rapid-acting analog) versus Huminsulin (a regular/NPH human insulin) are easy to confuse despite very different action profiles. Always check both the brand name and the molecule or type printed on the label before injecting.

### 19. What should I do if I am unsure which insulin type is in my pen or vial?
**Answer:** Do not guess or assume. Check the label for the molecule name (for example, aspart, glargine or NPH) and brand, compare it with your prescription, and ask your pharmacist or care team to confirm before using it.

### 20. Who decides which insulin molecule, brand and dose I should use?
**Answer:** Insulin is a Schedule H/H1 prescription medicine in India. The specific molecule, brand, strength and dose must always be chosen by your treating physician based on your individual glucose pattern, lifestyle, cost and access—not chosen independently or copied from another patient's regimen.

### 21. What rapid-acting insulin brands are used in the US market?
**Answer:** US rapid-acting brands include Admelog and Humalog/Humalog Pen/KwikPen (insulin lispro), Apidra/Apidra SoloStar (insulin glulisine), Fiasp/Fiasp FlexTouch (insulin aspart) and NovoLog (insulin aspart). These are the same rapid-acting molecules used in India, sold under different brand names.

### 22. What is Afrezza, and how is it different from other rapid-acting insulins?
**Answer:** Afrezza is an inhaled powder form of regular human insulin, breathed in at the start of a meal instead of injected. It is not appropriate for everyone—people with chronic lung disease such as asthma or COPD should not use it, and lung function is checked before and during treatment.

### 23. Which US brands provide short-acting (regular) human insulin?
**Answer:** Humulin R (including Humulin R Pen) and Novolin R are regular human insulin products used in the US, matching the same short-acting molecule as India's Actrapid, Huminsulin R and similar brands.

### 24. Which US brands provide intermediate-acting NPH insulin?
**Answer:** Humulin N and Novolin N are NPH (human insulin isophane suspension) products used in the US—the same intermediate-acting molecule as India's Insulatard, Huminsulin N and Insugen-N.

### 25. Which US brands provide long-acting basal insulin?
**Answer:** US long-acting basal brands include Basaglar KwikPen, Lantus/Lantus SoloStar and Toujeo/Toujeo Max (insulin glargine), Levemir (insulin detemir), and Tresiba FlexTouch (insulin degludec)—the same basal molecules marketed in India as Lantus, Basalog, Glaritus, Levemir and Tresiba.

### 26. Which US brands are premixed analog insulins?
**Answer:** Humalog Mix 75/25 and Humalog Mix 50/50 combine insulin lispro protamine suspension with insulin lispro injection; NovoLog Mix 70/30 combines insulin aspart protamine suspension with insulin aspart injection. These match India's Humalog Mix 25/50 and NovoMix 30.

### 27. What is Ryzodeg 70/30 and is it available under the same name in different countries?
**Answer:** Ryzodeg 70/30 (FlexTouch) combines 70% insulin degludec (long-acting) with 30% insulin aspart (rapid-acting) in one pen. It is marketed under the same brand name, Ryzodeg, in both the US and India, though availability can vary by country and pharmacy.

### 28. Which US brands are premixed human insulins?
**Answer:** Humulin 70/30 (including Humulin 70/30 KwikPen) and Novolin 70/30 combine 70% NPH human insulin with 30% regular human insulin—matching India's Human Mixtard 30, Huminsulin 30/70, Insuman Comb 30 and similar products.

### 29. Why do the same insulin molecules have different brand names in different countries?
**Answer:** Manufacturers license and market the same molecule under different trade names in different regions—for example, insulin glargine is sold as Lantus in both the US and India, but also as Basaglar in the US and Basalog or Glaritus in India. The molecule name, not the brand, tells you what the insulin actually is.

### 30. With so many brand names across countries, what is the single safest habit when handling insulin?
**Answer:** Always identify your insulin by its molecule name (for example, lispro, glargine, degludec or NPH) in addition to the brand name, and compare both against your prescription before every injection—regardless of which country's brand you are using.

### 31. Which Eli Lilly KwikPen products provide fast-acting or mealtime insulin?
**Answer:** Humalog KwikPen (available as U-100 and U-200, insulin lispro), Humalog Junior KwikPen (half-unit dosing) and Lyumjev KwikPen (insulin lispro-aabc, ultra-rapid-acting) are Eli Lilly's fast-acting, mealtime KwikPen options.

### 32. Which Eli Lilly KwikPen products provide intermediate, short-acting or premixed insulin?
**Answer:** Humulin N KwikPen (NPH, intermediate-acting) and Humulin R KwikPen (regular, short-acting) provide background or mealtime human insulin, while Humalog Mix75/25 KwikPen and Humalog Mix50/50 KwikPen are premixed lispro/lispro-protamine blends—together completing Eli Lilly's KwikPen insulin range.

### 33. Are all Lilly KwikPen-style pens insulin?
**Answer:** No. The same KwikPen device design is also used by Lilly for non-insulin medicines, including GLP-1/GIP receptor agonists used for type 2 diabetes and weight management, which are a different drug class dosed and monitored differently from insulin. Always check whether a pen contains insulin or a non-insulin medicine before use, and ask your pharmacist or care team if you are unsure.
